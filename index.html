Overview

SWE (Society of Women Engineers), GradSWE (Graduate Society of Women Engineers), AISES (American Indian Science and Engineering Society), NSBE (National Society of Black Engineers), and SOLES (Society of Latino Engineers and Scientists) cordially invite you to our our second annual Engineering for Good Research Symposium! The event aims to highlight cutting edge engineering research conducted at UCLA that is driven by humanitarian and social goals. This year, our lineup will feature Prof. Jenny Jay, Prof. Dino Di Carlo, and Prof. Sergio Carbajo. Through presentations, open Q&A, and networking, we will provide students with a chance to learn about some amazing research and connect with research teams! Food will be provided :)

Meet the Speakers

Sergio Carbajo

Electrical & Computer Engineering (ECE) Background
  • BS in Telecom Engineering from Tecnun, Universidad de Navarra, 2009
  • M.Sc. in Electrical and Computer Engineering from the National Science Foundation Engineering Research Center at Colorado State University, 2012
  • PhD in Physics from Massachusetts Institute of Technology and Deutsches Elektronen Synchrotron
Biography Sergio is the founder and director of the Quantum Light-Matter Cooperative, a research consortium at various institutions across California whose mission is to understand, design, and ultimately control light-driven physical processes to help solve interconnected socio-technological challenges. The cooperative's main research approach is to develop novel tools and frameworks to record the smallest, fastest, and most elusive constituents of nature in action. Jennifer Jay

Civil & Environmental Engineering (C&EE) Background
  • B.S., M.S., and Ph.D. in Civil and Environmental Engineering at Massachusetts Institute of Technology
Biography Professor Jay’s research integrates field and laboratory approaches to better understand the geochemical and microbial processes that govern the fate of contaminants in the environment. Specific interests include the geochemical and microbial methylation of mercury by sulfate-reducing bacteria (the end-product of this reaction, methylmercury, is a potent neurotoxin with a very strong tendency to bioaccumulate), the mobilization of arsenic in groundwater, and the persistence of fecal indicator bacteria and pathogens in beach sediment. Understanding the cycling of contaminants in aquatic systems allows us to better assess and minimize hazards associated with environmental contamination, and to more accurately predict effects of environmental perturbations. Professor Jay’s research evaluates how humans affect the world around us, along with determining ways to improve the world by examining the coastal water quality, heavy metals in the environment, and the environmental proliferation of antibiotic resistance. In addition she analyzes how environmental education can diminish the carbon footprint of dietary choices. Important Links

Dino di Carlo

Mechanical & Aerospace Engineering (MECH&AE) Background
  • B.S., University of California, Berkeley, 2002
  • Ph.D., University of California, Berkeley and San Francisco, 2006
  • Postdoctoral Training with Professor Mehmet Toner, Harvard Medical School, Massachusetts General Hospital, Center for Engineering in Medicine, 2006-2008
Biography Dino Di Carlo is a professor, inventor, and entrepreneur with >170 publications and >60 issued patents. He is the Armond and Elena Hairapetian Endowed Professor in Engineering and Medicine at UCLA, Vice Chair of the Bioengineering Department, and Deputy Director of the National Science Foundation Engineering Research Center focused on Precision Advanced Technologies and Health Systems for Underserved Populations. His academic research has focused at the interface of micro & nanosystems, data science, and the ultimate limits of biology (single cells and molecules). Over the last 10 years, Dr. Di Carlo co-founded seven startup companies in life science research tools, diagnostics, medical device, and pharmaceutical industries. Important Links
